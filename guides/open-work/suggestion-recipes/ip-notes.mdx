<Note>
**For patent counsel.** This is a self-contained summary and patent-evaluation note for the **leverage loop** — the one-click action that, given a target person, ranks everyone reachable in their network for introduction value and returns a ranked, explainable set of introductions. It documents the method, its candidate inventive elements, and the adjacent prior art, and is written to be read end-to-end without further context. *This is a technical description for assessment, not a legal opinion; nothing here is legal advice or a claim construction.*
</Note>

## Summary — the strategy

A **persona** is a person decomposed into eight orthogonal `(narrative, vector)` pairs — eight distinct questions, eight distinct source signals (who they are, what they know, what they can do, what they've done, what they talk about, how they talk, who they're with, what they care about). The loop scores a *target* persona against every *candidate* persona reachable in the graph and returns a ranked, explainable set of introductions, in one action.

The non-obvious part is not the embeddings. Cosine similarity between two profiles is a commodity. The method is in **how the eight signals are combined**, and the spine is a single claim: **the aggregation function _is_ the definition of a good introduction, so each dimension is transformed by its semantic role before any score is summed — not averaged uniformly.** Four moves follow from that:

<Steps>
<Step title="Role-typed aggregation, not uniform similarity">
The eight dimensions are not peers competing to be averaged. Three are **drivers** (the reasons to introduce — shared field, shared discourse, shared passion). Two are **valence** dimensions whose contribution *changes sign with intent*. Two are **modifiers** (fit, weighted near zero). One is **structural** and leaves the embedding space entirely. A naive mean treats all eight as equally good-when-high; this method does not.
</Step>
<Step title="Channel-aligned (diagonal) comparison">
The target's dimension *k* is compared only to the candidate's dimension *k* — domain↔domain, discourse↔discourse — never flat across the whole persona. Flat "row-vs-all" vector search compares incommensurable channels (how-they-talk against what-they-did) and destroys the orthogonality the eight dimensions were designed to preserve.
</Step>
<Step title="Graph-gated, non-monotonic brokerage">
Connectivity comes from the relationship graph, not the embedding. Already-connected pairs are **suppressed regardless of similarity** — they are not introductions. The valuable target is the *structural hole*: moderate distance, low neighbourhood overlap. The graph overrides the vector.
</Step>
<Step title="Off-diagonal serendipity with a lift metric">
A deliberate cross-channel pass surfaces the non-obvious intro — the candidate's *passion* matching the target's *profession*. It is scored as the gain over the diagonal (**lift**), which isolates exactly the matches the obvious comparison would have missed, and discards cross-hits that merely echo an already-strong diagonal.
</Step>
</Steps>

Two scoring **modes** — *peer* (like meets like, for war-stories) and *complement* (the missing piece, for founder↔investor) — run over the same data and produce different introduction *types* by flipping the valence sign. The output is not just a score: every suggestion carries **provenance** — the two specific narratives that explain it — which become the prompt for the drafted introduction.

## Why it works

The method rests on a specific theory of what a valuable introduction *is*. Not "two similar people" — similarity is cheap and mostly measures base rate, since everyone in a field resembles everyone else in it. A valuable introduction is an **arbitrage on the gap between latent affinity and realized connection**: a pair who *should* know each other and *don't*. Value concentrates where four properties hold at once — the affinity is **distinctive**, the connection is **absent**, the match fits the **intent**, and the pairing is **non-obvious**. Each component of the method exists to measure one of these, and none of the four is visible to a plain similarity score.

**Distinctive affinity — why standardization and spike-seeking pooling.** Raw cosine similarity is anisotropic and base-rate-laden: text embeddings sit in a narrow positive cone, and a 0.7 mostly reflects that two people are both, say, in tech. Z-scoring each facet against the reachable population converts "how similar" into "how *unusually* similar for this facet" — the surprise, not the level. (Same logic as TF-IDF: a facet is informative in proportion to how much it deviates from the population baseline; a facet on which everyone matches carries no signal, and standardization mutes it automatically.) The soft-max pool then rewards a *single strong thread* over diffuse resemblance, because an introduction needs a nameable reason — "you both did X" — and reasons are maxima, not averages. Diffuse 0.6-across-the-board similarity has no reason in it; it is base-rate overlap wearing a high mean.

**Absent connection — why the graph overrides the embedding.** The embedding measures *latent* affinity; only the graph measures *realized* connection. The value of an introduction is the difference between them — which neither signal computes alone. This is why brokerage is **non-monotonic in distance**: value ≈ predicted affinity × absence of an existing path, so it collapses at both ends. Too close (a direct edge, high neighbourhood overlap) and there is no gap to close — they are already connected, or a mutual contact will connect them without you. Too far and the affinity is speculative and the introducer has no context to make the introduction land. The value lives in the structural hole in the middle, a property of the graph, not the vectors — which is why a direct edge *suppresses* the suggestion regardless of similarity. Closing an already-closed gap has no value.

**Intent alignment — why role-typed aggregation and sign-inverting modes.** "Similarity is good" is false as a blanket rule, because value is goal-relative. A *peer* introduction (shared-experience, validation, camaraderie) is made more valuable by matched identity and trajectory; a *complement* introduction (a resource the other lacks) is made more valuable by identity and capability *distance* with domain proximity. The same measured facets serve both, because the facets are raw material and the *sign and weighting* encode the goal — which is how the method produces two distinct, internally-consistent introduction types from one computation by flipping a sign. A uniform average cannot represent "this facet should count positively here and negatively there," so it cannot express the actual structure of introduction value.

**Non-obviousness — why the off-diagonal lift.** The diagonal (field↔field, topics↔topics) finds matches that are *obvious* — which is precisely why they are low-value: an obvious match is one the parties, or a mutual contact, or a trivial algorithm would already surface. The earned value of a recommender is inverse to the obviousness of what it surfaces. The cross-facet pass finds the match invisible on the obvious axis — your profession is their weekend obsession — and the **lift** metric (`cross − diagonal`) isolates exactly the suggestions whose value is *not* already explained by an obvious shared thread, discarding cross-hits that merely echo a strong diagonal. The method explicitly optimizes for surfacing non-obvious connections.

<Info>
The same standardization that makes a match *informative* also makes it *legible*: because every score is a deviation from a population baseline, a high composite arrives already accompanied by *which facets* drove it — the provenance that seeds the drafted introduction. Usefulness and explainability fall out of one mechanism rather than being bolted on.
</Info>

**Why the obvious baseline fails.** Plain cosine-similarity top-k — the natural first approach — fails on all four properties at once: it measures base-rate similarity rather than distinctiveness, is blind to realized network structure (it will happily "introduce" people who already work together), is intent-agnostic (one ranking for every purpose), and surfaces only the obvious (the highest cosine is the most generic match). Each component of this method exists because a specific one of those failures destroys introduction quality, and the components are not separable: standardization without the graph still introduces already-connected people; the graph without role-typing still treats a complement as a failed peer; both without the off-diagonal pass still miss the non-obvious match that was the point. The composition is load-bearing: no single component suffices, and each exists because a specific failure of the others would otherwise destroy introduction quality.

### The method, as a numbered process

The leverage loop (**100**) proceeds as the following steps; the reference numerals correspond to **FIG. 1** below.

1. **(102) Decompose** each person into a fixed set of eight orthogonal `(narrative, vector)` dimensions, each generated from a distinct, named source-signal class and embedded independently — the substrate every later step depends on.
2. **(104) Resolve** the candidate set and the pairwise graph distance / neighbourhood overlap from the relationship graph, not a full-table vector scan, bounding both relevance and the standardization population.
3. **(106) Compare channel-aligned and standardize**: for each dimension *k*, compare the target's dimension *k* to the candidate's dimension *k*, then z-score each dimension's similarities across the candidate pool before any combination.
4. **(108 → 110) Gate on realized connection**: if the pair is directly connected or trivially connectable, suppress the candidate irrespective of similarity — it is not an introduction; otherwise continue.
5. **(112) Aggregate by semantic role**: pool the driver dimensions with a tunable mean→max (soft-max) operator; add the valence dimensions with a sign that inverts between peer and complement intent; substitute the structural-hole signal for the affiliation dimension's content similarity, with node-level brokerage admitted only as a bounded prior.
6. **(114) Off-diagonal pass**: within the topical sub-family only, compute the cross-dimension comparisons, select the winning cross per candidate, and score it as **lift** over that candidate's diagonal driver pool — isolating non-obvious matches and discarding cross-hits that merely echo a strong diagonal.
7. **(116) Rank and return**: order candidates by the composite and emit provenance-tagged introductions, each carrying the specific pair of narratives that explain it, from the single user action.

### Candidate inventive elements

<AccordionGroup>
<Accordion title="Role-typed aggregation with intent-dependent sign inversion" icon="diagram-project">
Dimensions are not combined uniformly: they are partitioned into driver / valence / modifier / structural roles, each with a different transform, and the *sign* of the valence and one modifier dimension is a function of the introduction intent (peer vs complement). The same standardized inputs yield two distinct ranked outputs. The inversion — rewarding *dissimilarity* on identity and operational capability to produce complementary matches — is the part least likely to be anticipated by similarity-maximizing recommenders.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="Graph-override-of-embedding with non-monotonic brokerage" icon="circle-nodes">
The relationship graph overrides the vector space on a per-dimension basis: connectivity gates the result (direct edge ⇒ suppression), and the affiliation dimension's *content* similarity is replaced by a *structural* signal that is non-monotonic in graph distance (peak at moderate distance / low overlap). The novelty is the explicit substitution and the non-monotonic preference, not link prediction per se.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="Constrained off-diagonal matching with a lift metric" icon="shuffle">
Cross-channel comparison restricted to a topical sub-family (a 3×3 block, not the full 8×8), scored as **lift over the diagonal** so that only matches the obvious comparison missed are surfaced, with the lift simultaneously suppressing trivially-correlated dimensions. The combination of (a) the family constraint, (b) winner-take-all selection, (c) the lift-over-diagonal objective, and (d) using the winning cell as generation provenance is the candidate claim.
</Accordion>
<Accordion title="The orthogonal-decomposition substrate itself" icon="layer-group">
A fixed eight-dimension persona where each dimension is tied to a distinct source-signal class and embedded independently, enabling channel-aligned comparison and per-dimension role assignment. Whether this is independently inventive or merely the enabling substrate for the above is a question for counsel; it may be better positioned as a dependent element.
</Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

### Adjacent art a search will surface

So counsel is not blindsided, the method sits near — and should be distinguished from — these well-trodden areas: collaborative-filtering and content-based recommenders; semantic / embedding similarity matching (including profile-to-profile matching in hiring and dating products); link prediction and graph representation learning (node2vec, GraphSAGE) and classical tie-strength / structural-hole / brokerage measures from network science; learning-to-rank; and "people you may know" / introduction features in professional networks. The defensible distance is the **specific composition**: role-typed, sign-inverting aggregation over an orthogonal narrative decomposition, with the graph overriding the embedding non-monotonically, plus the constrained lift-scored off-diagonal pass — rather than any single component, each of which has precedent.
