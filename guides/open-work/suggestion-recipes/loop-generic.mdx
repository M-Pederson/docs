The **leverage loop** is the one-click action that, given a target person, ranks everyone reachable in their network by introduction value and returns a ranked, explainable set of intros. This page is the method and its implementation.

<Info>
Strategy framing and the patent / IP write-up live in [**IP Notes**](/guides/open-work/suggestion-recipes/ip-notes); the evaluation harness is in [**loop_generic Harness Suggestions**](/guides/open-work/suggestion-recipes/harness-suggestions).
</Info>

## What a persona is

Eight was chosen as the orthogonal set: every dimension answers a distinct question from a distinct source signal. Fewer would compress unrelated concepts into one vector; more would overlap and dilute matching precision.

| # | Dimension | Question | Primary source signals | Role |
|---|-----------|----------|------------------------|------|
| 1 | `identity_profile` | Who are they right now? | Title, company, founder/exec status, signature project, headline | Valence |
| 2 | `domain_focus` | What do they know? | Expertise edges + industry/niche narrative | **Driver** |
| 3 | `soft_skills` | What can they do? | Synthesized from social signals, recommendations, peer mentions, track-record evidence | Modifier |
| 4 | `track_record` | What have they done? | Employment, founding, contributions, authorship, honors, certs | Valence |
| 5 | `public_engagement` | What do they talk about? | Posts, talks, content topics | **Driver** |
| 6 | `communication_style` | How do they talk about it? | Tone, format, audience, corpus analysis | Modifier |
| 7 | `community_affiliation` | Who are they with? | Tribes, scenes, ecosystems, org affiliations | Structural |
| 8 | `passions_interests` | What beyond work? | Causes, hobbies, side projects, advocacy | **Driver** |

Reads as a narrative: who → know → can do → have done → talk about → how → who with → what beyond.

---

## The core move: aggregation is the product

Given eight per-dimension similarities, the aggregator is a product decision before it is a math one. **Mean** says a good match is *broadly* similar. **Max** says it needs *one compelling thread*. They ship different products.

| Candidate | Eight scores | Mean | Max | Top-3 mean |
|-----------|--------------|------|-----|------------|
| One strong thread | `0.90`, then `0.40 × 7` | 0.46 | 0.90 | 0.57 |
| Diffusely alike | `0.60 × 8` | 0.60 | 0.60 | 0.60 |

Mean ranks the diffuse candidate first; max ranks the spike first. They disagree on who to introduce. For relationship introductions the spike is almost always the better call — one strong shared thread ("you both did *X*") is what makes an intro land, whereas diffuse 0.6 similarity is just "two generically alike people," a weak reason to put two names in an email.

So the bias is **max-leaning, not mean** — but raw max is fragile (one generically-phrased narrative spikes against everyone, and corroboration is discarded: five threads at 0.70 should beat one at 0.72). The method therefore uses a **soft-max (Boltzmann) pool with a temperature** — one parameter that slides continuously from mean (temp 0) to max (temp → ∞) — applied *only to the driver dimensions*, and tuned by evaluation rather than chosen by hand.

<Warning>
**Standardize per dimension before aggregating — this matters more than the aggregator choice.** Text-embedding cosine similarities are compressed and anisotropic: a 0.7 is not "70% match," and different dimensions baseline differently (everyone's `professional background` matches at ~0.6). Z-score each of the eight columns across the candidate pool *first*; then a high score means *standout for that facet*, which is what a weighted sum should reward. Without this, a high-baseline dimension dominates the mean and a high-variance one dominates the max — both for the wrong reasons.
</Warning>

## Three roles, not eight peers

The eight dimensions enter the score through different transforms:

| Dimension | High similarity means… | Role | Treatment |
|-----------|------------------------|------|-----------|
| `domain_focus` | shared field, instant common ground | **Driver** | high weight, soft-max pool |
| `public_engagement` | same things said publicly | **Driver** | high weight — strongest signal |
| `passions_interests` | they bond as humans, not transact | **Driver** | high weight (durable relationships) |
| `identity_profile` | same kind of person, same stage | **Valence** | sign flips by mode |
| `track_record` | parallel trajectories | **Valence** | sign flips by mode |
| `soft_skills` | same operational capabilities | **Modifier** | low weight; *inverted* in complement |
| `communication_style` | the intro will click | **Modifier** | low weight, tiebreaker |
| `community_affiliation` | same scene | **Structural** | non-monotonic + graph-gated |

The logic: you introduce two people *because* they share a domain, talk about the same things, or care about the same causes (2 / 5 / 8). You do **not** introduce them because they write in the same register (6) or both manage big teams (3). And `soft_skills` similarity is often *anti*-predictive — the best intros are complementary (visionary meets operator), not matched — which is why its sign inverts in complement mode.

<Info>
`passions_interests` sitting in the driver bucket is deliberate. For a platform selling *durable relationships* rather than transactional matches, the dimension that predicts stickiness should carry weight, not be an afterthought.
</Info>

## Diagonal matching (channel-aligned)

The base score is **dimension-aligned**: target.dim *k* ↔ candidate.dim *k*, eight parallel comparisons, seven through the vector space and one (`community_affiliation`) handed to the graph instead. A flat top-cosine search across all rows would score `domain_focus` against `passions_interests` — pure noise — and throw away the orthogonality the dimensions were built for.

**`community_affiliation` is non-monotonic, and the graph already knows the answer.** Very high scene-similarity usually means the two are *already connected or trivially connectable* — a low-value intro. Moderate overlap is the sweet spot: a bridgeable structural hole. Rather than fit an inverted-U on an embedding score, the method gates on actual graph distance: a direct edge **suppresses** the suggestion regardless of every other score, and the structural brokerage signal (moderate distance, low neighbourhood overlap) *replaces* the content-similarity of dimension 7. The embedding says "scene-adjacent"; the graph says "actually connected." The graph wins.

**`identity_profile` and `track_record` have no fixed sign.** A peer intro (founder ↔ founder) rewards high identity similarity; a complementary intro (founder ↔ investor) rewards identity *distance* with domain proximity. These are not blended into one number — the loop runs both modes and surfaces them as two intro *types* ("people like you" and "people you're missing" are different shelves).

## Serendipity (off-diagonal cross-block)

The diagonal finds people who are *obviously* alike. The serendipity layer finds the intro neither party would think to make: **the same subject surfacing in a different slot on the other person** — your `domain` ↔ their `passion`.

The discipline that makes this work: **do not score all 64 cross-cells.** Off-diagonal is only meaningful *within the topical family* — the dimensions that all encode subject matter through different lenses: `domain_focus` (subject you do for money), `public_engagement` (subject you broadcast), `passions_interests` (subject you love). Cross-family cells (topical ↔ `soft_skills` / `communication_style` / `identity_profile`) are dropped: they compare *how A talks* to *what B did*, an incoherent cosine where a high score is an artifact. So it is a **3×3 cross-block, not 8×8** — six off-diagonal cells.

Three principles:

1. **Winner-take-all.** You want one cross-pairing to light up *hard*, not diffuse cross-similarity — so **argmax**, not mean. The winning `(target_dim → candidate_dim)` pair names the two narratives to feed the LLM for the intro sentence.
2. **Lift, not raw cross-max.** If A and B already share a topic on the diagonal, the cross-cells fire too — same topic, double-counted. The valuable serendipity is the cross-hit the diagonal **missed**. So `serendipity_lift = cross_argmax − diagonal_driver_pool`, both in the same z-space. (This refines the first sketch, which subtracted the full composite; comparing the cross argmax against the diagonal *driver pool* keeps it apples-to-apples.)
3. **Direction is real.** `your domain ↔ their passion` and `your passion ↔ their domain` are different cells with different framing — who is the enthusiast versus the pro flips. Both are scored; the argmax picks the actual hook.

The lift metric does **double duty as a correlation guard**: if someone's passion and domain narratives are near-identical (their hobby *is* their job), their cross-cells inflate trivially — but so does their diagonal, so lift stays low and they are not falsely flagged. Free.

## The scoring pipeline

Heavy vector math stays in AlloyDB; the connectivity and bridge signal comes from FalkorDB; the composition logic lives in Go. The databases hand Go a small per-candidate matrix and Go does the cheap part.

<CodeGroup>

```sql AlloyDB · per-dimension z-scored matrix
-- one persona = 8 (narrative, vector) rows keyed by person_id
CREATE TABLE persona_vector (
  person_id  uuid         NOT NULL,
  dim        smallint     NOT NULL,          -- 1..8
  narrative  text,
  embedding  vector(3072) NOT NULL,          -- gemini-embedding-2, normalized at 3072
  valid_from timestamptz  NOT NULL DEFAULT now(),
  valid_to   timestamptz,                    -- bitemporal; NULL = current
  PRIMARY KEY (person_id, dim, valid_from)
);
-- ANN only needed when scoring beyond the ego network:
CREATE INDEX ON persona_vector USING scann (embedding cosine)
  WITH (num_leaves = 1000) WHERE valid_to IS NULL;

-- $1 = target person_id ; $2 = candidate_ids uuid[] (from FalkorDB)
WITH tgt AS (
  SELECT
    (SELECT embedding FROM persona_vector WHERE person_id=$1 AND dim=1 AND valid_to IS NULL) AS v1,
    (SELECT embedding FROM persona_vector WHERE person_id=$1 AND dim=2 AND valid_to IS NULL) AS v2,
    (SELECT embedding FROM persona_vector WHERE person_id=$1 AND dim=3 AND valid_to IS NULL) AS v3,
    (SELECT embedding FROM persona_vector WHERE person_id=$1 AND dim=4 AND valid_to IS NULL) AS v4,
    (SELECT embedding FROM persona_vector WHERE person_id=$1 AND dim=5 AND valid_to IS NULL) AS v5,
    (SELECT embedding FROM persona_vector WHERE person_id=$1 AND dim=6 AND valid_to IS NULL) AS v6,
    (SELECT embedding FROM persona_vector WHERE person_id=$1 AND dim=8 AND valid_to IS NULL) AS v8
    -- dim 7 (community) intentionally NOT scored by embedding — handled by the graph
),
sims AS (                          -- cosine sim = 1 - (<=>), valid because 3072-dim is normalized
  SELECT c.person_id,
    1-(p1.embedding<=>t.v1) AS s_identity,
    1-(p2.embedding<=>t.v2) AS s_domain,
    1-(p3.embedding<=>t.v3) AS s_soft,
    1-(p4.embedding<=>t.v4) AS s_track,
    1-(p5.embedding<=>t.v5) AS s_public,
    1-(p6.embedding<=>t.v6) AS s_comm,
    1-(p8.embedding<=>t.v8) AS s_passion,
    -- topical off-diagonal cells (3x3 family {2,5,8}); reuses joined vectors
    1-(p5.embedding<=>t.v2) AS x_dom_pub,
    1-(p8.embedding<=>t.v2) AS x_dom_pas,
    1-(p2.embedding<=>t.v5) AS x_pub_dom,
    1-(p8.embedding<=>t.v5) AS x_pub_pas,
    1-(p2.embedding<=>t.v8) AS x_pas_dom,
    1-(p5.embedding<=>t.v8) AS x_pas_pub
  FROM unnest($2::uuid[]) AS c(person_id)
  CROSS JOIN tgt t
  JOIN persona_vector p1 ON p1.person_id=c.person_id AND p1.dim=1 AND p1.valid_to IS NULL
  JOIN persona_vector p2 ON p2.person_id=c.person_id AND p2.dim=2 AND p2.valid_to IS NULL
  JOIN persona_vector p3 ON p3.person_id=c.person_id AND p3.dim=3 AND p3.valid_to IS NULL
  JOIN persona_vector p4 ON p4.person_id=c.person_id AND p4.dim=4 AND p4.valid_to IS NULL
  JOIN persona_vector p5 ON p5.person_id=c.person_id AND p5.dim=5 AND p5.valid_to IS NULL
  JOIN persona_vector p6 ON p6.person_id=c.person_id AND p6.dim=6 AND p6.valid_to IS NULL
  JOIN persona_vector p8 ON p8.person_id=c.person_id AND p8.dim=8 AND p8.valid_to IS NULL
)
SELECT s.person_id,
  -- standardize each diagonal dimension across the candidate pool
  COALESCE((s_identity-avg(s_identity)OVER())/NULLIF(stddev_pop(s_identity)OVER(),0),0) AS z_identity,
  COALESCE((s_domain  -avg(s_domain)  OVER())/NULLIF(stddev_pop(s_domain)  OVER(),0),0) AS z_domain,
  COALESCE((s_soft    -avg(s_soft)    OVER())/NULLIF(stddev_pop(s_soft)    OVER(),0),0) AS z_soft,
  COALESCE((s_track   -avg(s_track)   OVER())/NULLIF(stddev_pop(s_track)   OVER(),0),0) AS z_track,
  COALESCE((s_public  -avg(s_public)  OVER())/NULLIF(stddev_pop(s_public)  OVER(),0),0) AS z_public,
  COALESCE((s_comm    -avg(s_comm)    OVER())/NULLIF(stddev_pop(s_comm)    OVER(),0),0) AS z_comm,
  COALESCE((s_passion -avg(s_passion) OVER())/NULLIF(stddev_pop(s_passion) OVER(),0),0) AS z_passion,
  -- raw cross cells; z-scored per type in Go (baselines differ across cell types)
  x_dom_pub, x_dom_pas, x_pub_dom, x_pub_pas, x_pas_dom, x_pas_pub,
  m.brokerage AS node_brokerage
FROM sims s
LEFT JOIN person_metrics m ON m.person_id = s.person_id;
```

```cypher FalkorDB · connectivity gate + pairwise bridge
// $a, $b person ids. node-level brokerage is a weak PRIOR; the intro-specific
// signal is the DYAD: do A and B share few ties, so the intro bridges a hole.
MATCH (a:Person {id:$a}), (b:Person {id:$b})
OPTIONAL MATCH sp = shortestPath((a)-[*..4]-(b))
WITH a, b, coalesce(length(sp), 99) AS dist
OPTIONAL MATCH (a)--(x)--(b)                       // mutual ties
WITH a, b, dist, count(DISTINCT x) AS shared
MATCH (a)--(na) WITH a, b, dist, shared, count(DISTINCT na) AS deg_a
MATCH (b)--(nb) WITH dist, shared, deg_a, count(DISTINCT nb) AS deg_b
RETURN dist,
       toFloat(shared) / NULLIF(deg_a + deg_b - shared, 0) AS jaccard;  // low = strong bridge
```

```go Go · composition, modes, and the two lanes
package match

import "math"

type Mode int

const (
	Peer       Mode = iota // founder ↔ founder — reward parallel identity/track
	Complement             // founder ↔ investor — reward distance + domain proximity
)

type DimZ struct {
	Identity, Domain, Soft, Track, Public, Comm, Passion float64
}

type CrossCell struct {
	TgtDim, CandDim int     // 8,2 = target's passion vs candidate's domain
	Raw             float64 // cosine from SQL, z-scored across the pool here
}

type Cand struct {
	ID            string
	Z             DimZ        // z-scored diagonal dims from SQL
	Cross         []CrossCell // 6 topical off-diagonal cells
	NodeBrokerage float64     // node-level prior
	Dist          int         // shortest-path hops; 0/1 => already connected
	Jaccard       float64     // neighbourhood overlap
}

type Result struct {
	ID                  string
	Diagonal            float64 // role-typed composite
	SerZ                float64 // best cross-cell, z-scored
	SerTgt, SerCand     int     // provenance: which two narratives explain it
	Lift                float64 // SerZ - diagonal driver pool
	Dropped             bool    // gated: already connected
}

const (
	wDrivers, wValence, wBridge = 0.55, 0.20, 0.15
	wSoft, wComm                = 0.03, 0.02
	softmaxTemp                 = 2.0 // 0 -> mean of drivers ; higher -> max. Tune by eval.
	nodeNudge                   = 0.3 // pairwise dominates; node prior only nudges
)

// Boltzmann soft-max: mean (temp 0) -> max (temp→∞). Defined on z-scores
// (which can be negative), unlike a power mean — that's why we use it post-standardization.
func softMax(temp float64, xs ...float64) float64 {
	if temp <= 1e-6 {
		var s float64
		for _, x := range xs {
			s += x
		}
		return s / float64(len(xs))
	}
	var num, den float64
	for _, x := range xs {
		w := math.Exp(temp * x)
		num, den = num+w*x, den+w
	}
	if den == 0 {
		return 0
	}
	return num / den
}

// Non-monotonic in distance: adjacent = no intro; far = no leverage;
// 2–3 hops with low overlap = the structural hole worth bridging.
func pairBridge(dist int, jaccard float64) float64 {
	switch dist {
	case 2:
		return 1.0 * (1 - jaccard)
	case 3:
		return 0.6 * (1 - jaccard)
	case 4:
		return 0.25 * (1 - jaccard)
	}
	return 0
}

func ScoreBatch(cs []Cand, mode Mode) []Result {
	// standardize cross-source signals into the same z-space as the dims
	bridge := make([]float64, len(cs))
	nodeB := make([]float64, len(cs))
	for i, c := range cs {
		bridge[i], nodeB[i] = pairBridge(c.Dist, c.Jaccard), c.NodeBrokerage
	}
	zBridge, zNode := zscore(bridge), zscore(nodeB)

	out := make([]Result, len(cs))
	for i, c := range cs {
		r := Result{ID: c.ID}
		if c.Dist <= 1 { // already connected — not an introduction
			r.Dropped = true
			out[i] = r
			continue
		}
		drivers := softMax(softmaxTemp, c.Z.Domain, c.Z.Public, c.Z.Passion)

		var valence, softSign float64 = 0, 1
		switch mode {
		case Peer:
			valence = c.Z.Identity + c.Z.Track
		case Complement:
			valence = c.Z.Domain - c.Z.Identity
			softSign = -1 // complementary skills beat matched skills
		}
		modifiers := wSoft*softSign*c.Z.Soft + wComm*c.Z.Comm

		r.Diagonal = wDrivers*drivers + wValence*valence +
			wBridge*(zBridge[i]+nodeNudge*zNode[i]) + modifiers

		// serendipity: argmax over z-scored topical cross-cells; lift vs driver pool
		best := math.Inf(-1)
		for _, cell := range zCross(cs, i) {
			if cell.z > best {
				best, r.SerTgt, r.SerCand = cell.z, cell.tgt, cell.cand
			}
		}
		r.SerZ = best
		r.Lift = best - drivers // same z-space => isolates the non-obvious match
		out[i] = r
	}
	return out
}

// Ranking: sort Diagonal desc for the obvious lane; sort Lift desc and filter
// Lift > tau for the serendipity lane; min-max each lane and take the per-candidate
// max for a single "overall" list. The winning (SerTgt, SerCand) pair, or the top
// diagonal dimensions, feed the LLM that drafts the introduction line.

func zscore(xs []float64) []float64 {
	n := float64(len(xs))
	if n == 0 {
		return xs
	}
	var mean float64
	for _, x := range xs {
		mean += x
	}
	mean /= n
	var v float64
	for _, x := range xs {
		v += (x - mean) * (x - mean)
	}
	sd := math.Sqrt(v / n)
	out := make([]float64, len(xs))
	if sd == 0 {
		return out
	}
	for i, x := range xs {
		out[i] = (x - mean) / sd
	}
	return out
}

// zCross standardizes each cross-cell TYPE (e.g. passion→domain) across the whole
// candidate pool, then returns this candidate's standardized cells, so the argmax
// compares like with like rather than always picking the highest-baseline cell type.
```

</CodeGroup>

<Tip>
**What is load-bearing, and what to verify against the stack.**

- The `1-(<=>)` identity holds **only because 3072-dim gemini-embedding-2 is normalized**. Truncating via MRL auto-normalizes (still fine); any cross-model mixing breaks it.
- The z-score reference pool is the **ego network** — "standout among reachable people," which is what's wanted. If `soft`/`comm` drift from being standardized over a domain-biased pool, precompute network-wide μ/σ per dimension instead of the windowed `OVER()`.
- **Do not lean on node brokerage** — it is the small nudge, not the signal. The pairwise Jaccard is what tells you the intro bridges a hole.
- Check the **inter-narrative correlation among the target's own eight vectors**. If `domain`/`public`/`passion` are themselves correlated, the soft-max over them double-counts and the driver bucket is narrower than it looks. Whiten or diversify the narrative prompts before trusting the pool.
- **Every weight and `softmaxTemp` is a hypothesis**, not a constant. They encode "discourse/domain/passion drive, skills/style barely register, community is graph-gated." Freeze them only after labeling intros you would and wouldn't make and grid-searching against acceptance.
</Tip>

## Interactive tuner

The demonstrator below runs the **actual scoring function** on nine synthetic candidates. Drag the controls and watch candidates reorder and move between lanes. Click any card for the full per-dimension breakdown.

What to look for:

- **Soft-max temperature.** At 0 the drivers average; push it up and a candidate with a single strong thread (the podcast host, all `public_engagement`) climbs as one spike starts to win.
- **Mode.** Flip *Peer → Complement* and the diagonal reorders: the fellow-founder (high `identity`/`track`) sinks, the investor (domain proximity, identity distance) rises. Same data, a different shelf of introductions.
- **Serendipity lift floor τ.** The fintech engineer with the weekend music project has a weak diagonal but the strongest cross-hit (`passion → your domain`). Raise τ and the merely-echoing cross-hits drop away, leaving only the genuinely non-obvious intro.
- **Bridge vs node-broker nudge.** The super-connector has a high node prior but high neighbourhood overlap — already scene-adjacent — so the pairwise bridge stays low and they rank poorly. Crank the node nudge to see the failure mode where connector-status wrongly dominates.
- **Suppressed strip.** The direct contact is gated out regardless of a strong content match — already connected is not an introduction.

## Open questions / next

- **Aggregation across the target's own eight is itself a design axis** — if the driver narratives correlate, diversify the prompts before trusting the soft-max pool.
- **Mutuality.** Introductions are bidirectional; the production scorer should combine target→candidate and candidate→target (e.g. `min` or harmonic mean) so the other party benefits too. The demonstrator scores one direction for clarity.
- **Eval is the unlock.** Label ~50 introductions you would and wouldn't make, grid-search temperature / weights / τ against acceptance, and freeze. Until then every constant on this page is a starting hypothesis.
- **Candidate B as eight narratives, not one vector.** If both sides are full personas, the off-diagonal pass becomes a per-facet assignment (max-match) rather than a single cross — a natural extension of the same mechanism.
